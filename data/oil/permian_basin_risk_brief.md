# Permian Basin, Texas — The "Assumed Backstop" That's Quietly Cracking

## Scenario Context

A Strait of Hormuz disruption removes ~17–21 Mbbl/day of global crude flow — roughly 20% of world supply. Every disruption model points to the same backstop: the Permian Basin (~45% of US oil, ~6.7 Mbbl/day). Three active constraints may prevent this assumption from holding.

<!-- depth:1 -->

All three risk vectors below are **currently active, not hypothetical**. A production ramp to offset Hormuz disruptions intensifies all three simultaneously — the very act of ramping is a trigger mechanism.

**Core scenario**: the world assumes Permian ramps during a Hormuz crisis. An ERCOT grid failure or pipeline constraint could prevent that ramp at exactly the wrong moment.

## Permian Field Operations

Three active vulnerabilities threaten field-level output across the Delaware and Midland sub-basins.

<!-- depth:1 -->

### Risk Vector 1 — Power Infrastructure Failure

**Status**: Active structural vulnerability; buildout delayed

The Permian Basin cannot import adequate electricity to sustain current production levels under stress. West Texas transmission additions are behind schedule.

- ERCOT reserve margin deficit (Summer 2026): ~3.7 GW
- Field operations electrical requirement: ~4.2 GW at 6.7 Mbbl/day
- Curtailment scenario: 5-day ERCOT load-shed → −850 Kbbl/day

### Risk Vector 2 — Subsurface Pressure / Zombie Well Crisis

**Status**: Active, regulatory enforcement underway

Texas regulators warn that wastewater re-injection is causing widespread underground pressure increases. Railroad Commission of Texas issuing non-compliance notices to Chevron, BP, and Coterra.

- Delaware Basin injection volumes: ~18.4 Mbbl/day
- Legacy inactive wells: ~120,000
- Orphaned / inadequately sealed wells: ~8,400

### Risk Vector 3 — Gas Pipeline Bottleneck (Waha Hub)

**Status**: Active constraint — ongoing as of March 2026

Waha natural gas hub prices have been negative on 38 out of 51 days in 2026. Blackcomb pipeline — the planned egress relief valve — pushed from July to **November 2026**.

- Negative Waha days in 2026: 38/51 (AEGIS Hedging)
- Blackcomb delay window with no new egress: July–November 2026
- Incremental gas from a ramp attempt: 0.8–1.2 Bcf/day above current egress capacity

<!-- depth:2 -->

### Detailed Analysis — Power Grid Exposure

**Key warning** *(U.S. News & World Report)*:
> "The greatest risk of failure for the Permian Basin Reliability Plan is for these necessary projects to falter under any delay."

**ERCOT exposure**: Summer 2026 peak demand projections show a 3.7 GW reserve margin deficit if West Texas transmission additions slip. Without new import capacity, an ERCOT demand spike triggers automatic load-shedding that hits industrial field customers — compressors and pumps — before residential consumers.

**Trigger scenario**: A summer 2026 heat wave or winter storm forces ERCOT emergency load-shed. Field compressors trip across the basin. A 5-day curtailment reduces Permian output by ~850 Kbbl/day — exactly the capacity Hormuz disruption models rely on.

**Mitigation gap**: Diesel backup could provide partial coverage, but sourcing 450 MW of mobile gensets during a crisis takes weeks. Uncertainty level: **HIGH**.

Financial exposure: $60M–$280M per large integrated operator (5-day curtailment at WTI $75–90/bbl).

### Detailed Analysis — Zombie Well Pressure

**Mechanism**: Wastewater re-injected into formations stressed by decades of extraction migrates through fault networks into legacy well casings. Zombie wells become pressure conduits connecting the deep injection zone to the surface. As injection volumes increase to match production ramp, risk scales nonlinearly.

**Trigger scenario**: A production ramp increases injection volumes proportionally. Pressure propagation accelerates. Railroad Commission issues broad shutdown orders across active injection zones, directly curtailing oil production.

Blowout incidents recorded 2022–2024: 3 confirmed. Uncertainty level: **CRITICAL** — zombie well spatial distribution is poorly mapped. Pressure propagation in fractured basement rock cannot be modelled at sufficient resolution.

Financial exposure: $32M–$190M (shutdown order scope + cleanup liability).

### Detailed Analysis — Waha Gas Bottleneck

**The ramp problem**: A Hormuz-response production increase adds 0.8–1.2 Bcf/day of incremental associated gas. Current spare egress cannot absorb this. Flaring headroom is constrained by Railroad Commission permit limits.

**Trigger scenario**: Operators asked to ramp output in Q3 2026. Associated gas spikes. Waha goes further negative. Flaring permits hit ceiling. Incremental oil curtailed by the gas it produces — the backstop fails precisely when needed.

Uncertainty level: **HIGH** — Blackcomb delay to November is confirmed; curtailment magnitude depends on scale/duration of the external demand shock.

Financial exposure: $42M–$155M per large operator (July–October curtailment window).

## Midstream Egress

Pipeline transport and storage connecting Permian production to Gulf Coast export terminals and the Cushing, Oklahoma hub.

<!-- depth:1 -->

### Gulf Coast Pipeline Integrity

**Status**: Deferred inspection risk; active monitoring gaps

The 847-mile mainline from Permian to Gulf Coast carries the majority of export-bound crude. ILI surveys on Segments A/B have flagged anomalies; the eastern corridor has a 4-year survey gap.

- ILI Type-B anomalies on western segments: 14 (require re-inspection within 12 months)
- Eastern corridor last full ILI survey: 2019
- High-consequence area crossings without recent survey: 3

### Cushing Storage Congestion

**Status**: Latent — activates under Hormuz ramp scenario

Cushing hub (~94M-barrel capacity) currently at 55–65% utilisation. A Hormuz-driven surge routing incremental Permian barrels north would push Cushing toward operational limits.

- Current utilisation: ~58% (EIA weekly stocks)
- Absorption capacity for incremental ramp: 8–12 Mbbl over 30 days
- Break-even carry cost: ~$1.40/bbl/month

<!-- depth:2 -->

### Detailed Analysis — Pipeline Integrity

**Trigger scenario**: Rapid throughput increase (Hormuz ramp) stresses pipe walls at anomaly locations. A failure at a coastal HCA crossing triggers a PHMSA emergency order restricting flow. Uncertainty level: **HIGH** — the 2019 survey gap means failure probability cannot be accurately bounded without new ILI data.

Financial exposure: $12M–$95M (repair + throughput loss during shutdown).

### Detailed Analysis — Cushing Congestion

**Mechanism**: As tanks approach capacity, operators face contango carry costs and are forced to discount crude to attract storage. Beyond ~85% utilisation, WTI pricing dislocates from global benchmarks — undermining the ramp economics that caused the congestion in the first place.

Uncertainty level: **MODERATE** — EIA stock data provides weekly visibility, but congestion threshold depends on simultaneous flows from multiple basins not fully observable in advance.

Financial exposure: $18M–$140M (contango carry cost + forced discount for a large operator).

## GOM Offshore Buffer

Supplemental Gulf of Mexico offshore production across a 17-platform fleet, designed to provide surge capacity during Permian constraints.

<!-- depth:1 -->

### Hurricane Platform Exposure

**Status**: Elevated — three platforms exceed original design envelope

Three high-risk platforms were designed to Cat 3 standards and have not been structurally upgraded. Operator insurance underwriter models classify them as non-compliant for a direct Cat 3+ strike.

- Platforms at risk: 3 of 17
- Combined production capacity at risk: ~47,000 bbl/day
- Last structural survey: 2021 (overdue — Cat 4 Ida now used as baseline scenario)

### Coastal Subsidence Risk

**Status**: Active monitoring gap — survey overdue

Gulf Coast pipeline approach segments have accumulated 2.4–4.8 inches of unaccounted subsidence movement since the last full survey in 2022. Eastern offshore approach crosses a high-subsidence zone documented by NOAA tide gauges.

<!-- depth:2 -->

### Detailed Analysis — Hurricane Exposure

**Seasonal window**: June–November. Peak risk: August–October.

**Trigger scenario**: A Cat 3+ direct hit on the eastern platform cluster during a Hormuz ramp removes the GOM buffer capacity exactly when it is needed as a failsafe. Insurance settlement: 6–18 months. Production restoration: 12–24 months.

Uncertainty level: **HIGH** — storm track forecasting at 6-month horizon has low skill; platform structural assessment is 5 years old.

Financial exposure: $20M–$180M per event (production loss + deductible; excludes platform replacement for total-loss scenario).

### Detailed Analysis — Coastal Subsidence

**Mechanism**: Subsidence at Gulf Coast pipe crossings induces bending stress on rigid pipe segments. At 4+ inches of differential movement, stress at bend points approaches yield threshold. A seismic event (common in induced-seismicity zones) can cause brittle failure.

**Trigger scenario**: Production ramp increases throughput pressure. A minor seismic event (M2.5+) triggers a stress fracture at a subsidence-stressed crossing. PHMSA classifies the failure as an HCA release; full corridor shutdown follows pending re-inspection.

Uncertainty level: **CRITICAL** — 4 years of unmonitored subsidence means the current pipe stress state is unknown. A re-survey is required before production ramp should be attempted on this corridor.

Financial exposure: $4M–$67M (repair + throughput rerouting; eastern approach only).

## Compounding Risk Assessment

<!-- depth:1 -->

These three vectors are not independent. All three Step 1 risks intensify under the same condition — a large Permian production ramp:

- Increases electrical load on a constrained grid (Vector 1)
- Increases wastewater injection volumes into pressurised formations (Vector 2)
- Increases associated gas volumes into a pipeline system with no spare egress (Vector 3)

A ramp attempt is not a safe hedge. It is a trigger mechanism for all three risks simultaneously. The midstream corridor and GOM buffer provide no reliable failsafe — both carry active vulnerabilities that worsen under surge conditions.

| Scenario | Trigger | Output Impact | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pipeline failure | Cushing/coastal crossing | −150–400 Kbbl/day | 14–60 days |
